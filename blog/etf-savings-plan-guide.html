An ETF savings plan is the single most powerful investment tool available to ordinary people. It is simple, low-cost, and has helped millions of Europeans build significant wealth over time — without picking stocks, timing the market, or having any financial expertise.

In this guide, you will learn everything you need to know to start your own ETF savings plan from scratch, with real numbers and specific recommendations for 2025.

1. What Is an ETF Savings Plan?

An ETF savings plan (German: ETF-Sparplan) is an automatic investment arrangement where a fixed amount of money is invested into one or more ETFs on a regular basis — typically monthly. Once you set it up, the investment happens automatically, without you needing to do anything.

Let us break down the two components:

What is an ETF?

An ETF (Exchange Traded Fund) is a basket of hundreds or thousands of stocks bundled into a single investment product. Instead of buying Apple, Microsoft and Amazon separately, you buy one ETF that holds all of them — plus hundreds more. The most popular ETF for long-term investors is the MSCI World ETF, which tracks approximately 1,600 companies across 23 developed countries.

💡 Key insight: One MSCI World ETF gives you instant ownership of Apple, Microsoft, Nestlé, Toyota, Samsung and 1,595 more of the world's most successful companies — for as little as €1 per month.

What makes it a savings plan?

The "savings plan" part means this investment happens automatically and repeatedly. You choose your ETF, your monthly amount, and your execution date — and then the broker automatically buys ETF shares for you every month, regardless of whether markets are up or down.

This is not a savings account. Your money is invested in real assets that fluctuate in value. However, historically, diversified ETF portfolios have grown significantly over time — making them far superior to savings accounts for long-term wealth building.

2. How Does an ETF Savings Plan Work?

Understanding how an ETF savings plan works helps you stick with it during the inevitable periods of market volatility. Here are the three key mechanisms:

Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

When you invest a fixed amount monthly, you automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer shares when prices are high. This mechanical strategy — called dollar-cost averaging — means you never invest everything at the worst possible moment.

📊 Example: You invest €200 per month in a MSCI World ETF. In Month 1, shares cost €80 — you buy 2.5 shares. In Month 2, markets fall and shares cost €65 — you automatically buy 3.08 shares. Your average cost per share is lower than the average market price. This is the DCA advantage.

Compound Interest

The returns you earn on your ETF are reinvested — meaning next year you earn returns on your returns. Over decades, this creates an exponential growth curve that Albert Einstein allegedly called "the eighth wonder of the world."

Automation removes emotion

The most important feature of an ETF savings plan is what it prevents: emotional decisions. Studies consistently show that investors who try to time the market — buying when it feels good, selling when it falls — earn significantly less than those who invest automatically every month, regardless of conditions.

🧠 The DALBAR finding: The average investor earns 3 to 4% less per year than the funds they invest in — entirely due to emotional decisions like panic selling. An automated savings plan removes these decisions entirely.

3. Which ETF Should You Choose?

For the vast majority of beginners, the answer is simple: a MSCI World ETF. Here is everything you need to know to choose the right one.

What to look for in an ETF

  • TER (Total Expense Ratio): The annual cost, expressed as a percentage. Lower is better. Target below 0.25% per year.
  • AUM (Assets Under Management): The fund size. Larger funds are more stable. Look for above €500 million.
  • Replication method: Physical replication means the ETF actually holds the underlying stocks. This is more transparent and safer.
  • Distribution type: Accumulating (Acc) ETFs reinvest dividends automatically — better for wealth building. Distributing (Dist) pays dividends as cash.
  • Domicile: Ireland-domiciled ETFs (ISIN starting with IE) are most tax-efficient for European investors.

Should you add Emerging Markets?

The classic "80/20 portfolio" adds 20% in Emerging Markets (MSCI EM) alongside 80% MSCI World. This gives exposure to fast-growing economies like China, India, Brazil and Taiwan. A good EM ETF for savings plans is EMIM (iShares MSCI Emerging Markets IMI, TER 0.18%). For absolute beginners, starting with MSCI World alone is perfectly fine.

4. How Much Should You Invest?

The most common question — and the most important thing to understand is this: the amount matters far less than starting. Even €25 per month invested consistently for 40 years at 7% return grows to over €65,000. The best amount is whatever you can commit to without needing to stop.

A practical framework

  • First priority: Build an emergency fund of 3 to 6 months of expenses in a separate high-yield savings account. Never invest money you might need within the next 3 years.
  • Starting amount: €25 to €100 per month is a great starting point. You can always increase it.
  • Target savings rate: Aim to invest 10 to 20% of your monthly income. More is better — the savings rate is the most powerful lever you control.
  • Never over-invest: Do not invest money you need for upcoming expenses. Short-term needs should stay in cash.

📊 The 10-year delay cost: Starting at 25 with €200/month at 7% annual return produces €524,000 by age 65. Starting at 35 produces €243,000. The 10-year delay costs €281,000 — from investing only €24,000 less in total. Start now, not later.

6. How to Set Up an ETF Savings Plan (Step by Step)

Setting up your first ETF savings plan takes approximately 10 minutes after your broker account is verified. Here is exactly how to do it:

Step 1: Open and verify your broker account

Download the Trade Republic or Scalable Capital app. Create an account with your email and phone number. Verify your identity with your passport or ID using the in-app process. Verification typically completes within 1 to 2 business days.

Step 2: Set up your Freistellungsauftrag (German investors)

This critical step is often skipped. In your broker app, go to Account → Tax → Freistellungsauftrag. Enter €1,000 (or €2,000 for couples). This assigns your annual tax-free investment profit allowance to your broker, saving you hundreds of euros per year automatically.

💡 Do not skip this: The Freistellungsauftrag gives you €1,000 per year in completely tax-free investment profits. Without it, your broker withholds 26.375% tax from the first euro of profit. It takes 2 minutes to set up and could save you hundreds of euros.

Step 3: Search for your ETF

In the app, search for IWDA (or XDWD or LCUW). Select the iShares Core MSCI World UCITS ETF USD (Acc) result. Verify it is the accumulating version — you will see "(Acc)" in the name.

Step 4: Create the savings plan

Tap on "Savings Plan" or "Sparplan." Enter your monthly amount. Choose your execution date — the 1st or 15th of the month both work well. Confirm. The plan is now active.

Step 5: Leave it alone

This is the most important step. Do not check it every day. Do not pause it when markets fall. Review it quarterly — check the allocation is still correct, confirm the plan is running, do nothing else. Let compound interest work.

7. Tax Basics You Need to Know

Understanding the basics of how ETFs are taxed in Germany (and most of the EU) will help you make better decisions and avoid costly mistakes.

Abgeltungsteuer

Investment profits in Germany are subject to Abgeltungsteuer at 25%, plus solidarity surcharge, bringing the effective rate to approximately 26.375%. This tax applies when you sell ETF shares at a profit, and to dividends from distributing ETFs.

Accumulating ETFs and the Vorabpauschale

Since 2018, accumulating ETFs are subject to a notional annual tax called the Vorabpauschale — a small annual tax on hypothetical gains, even if you have not sold anything. This is calculated automatically and is typically very small. It does not eliminate the long-term tax advantages of accumulating ETFs.

Sparerpauschbetrag

Every German taxpayer has a €1,000 annual tax-free allowance on investment income (€2,000 for couples). Set up a Freistellungsauftrag with your broker to use this automatically. This alone covers most beginners' annual investment profits in the early years.

8. The 5 Most Common ETF Savings Plan Mistakes

  1. Pausing during market crashes. This is the most costly mistake. When markets fall 20%, your savings plan is automatically buying more shares at discounted prices. Pausing it destroys exactly this advantage. Never stop — especially during crashes.
  2. Choosing a high-cost ETF. A 1.5% TER versus 0.20% TER on €100,000 over 30 years costs you over €200,000 in lost wealth. Always check the TER before buying. Target below 0.25%.
  3. Investing money you might need soon. If you have no emergency fund and invest everything, any unexpected expense forces you to sell — potentially at the worst moment. Build 3 to 6 months of expenses in cash first.
  4. Switching ETFs based on recent performance. Last year's best-performing ETF is not next year's. Chasing performance consistently produces below-average returns. Choose your ETF, stay with it.
  5. Not setting up a Freistellungsauftrag. German investors who skip this pay 26.375% tax from the first euro of profit. Takes 2 minutes to fix. Do it today.

9. Frequently Asked Questions

How much money do I need to start an ETF savings plan? ▾
Most European brokers like Trade Republic and Scalable Capital let you start with as little as €1 per month. A practical starting point for meaningful wealth building is €25 to €100 per month. Remember: the amount matters far less than starting.
Which ETF is best for a savings plan in 2025? ▾
For most beginners, the MSCI World ETF is the best choice. Top options include IWDA (iShares, TER 0.20%), XDWD (Xtrackers, TER 0.19%) and LCUW (Amundi, TER 0.12%). All three are physically replicating, accumulating, Ireland-domiciled and extremely low cost. Pick any one — the differences between them are minimal.
Is an ETF savings plan safe? ▾
Your ETF shares are held in a segregated custody account, completely separate from the broker's own assets. Even if the broker went bankrupt, your investments are protected and transferable to another broker. The market value will fluctuate — sometimes significantly. This is normal and expected. Over long periods, globally diversified ETFs have historically always recovered from declines and reached new highs.
Can I pause or stop my ETF savings plan? ▾
Yes — you can pause, change the amount, or stop your savings plan at any time in your broker app. However, pausing during market downturns is one of the most common and costly mistakes. Market crashes are exactly when your plan is buying more shares at discounted prices. The optimal approach is to never pause — or even to increase the amount during downturns if possible.
How long should I run an ETF savings plan? ▾
The longer the better. The real power of an ETF savings plan comes from compound interest over time. A minimum of 10 years is recommended — and the mathematics show that the last 10 years of a 40-year savings plan typically produce more wealth than the first 30 years combined. The ideal answer: run it until you reach your financial independence number.
What is the difference between accumulating and distributing ETFs? ▾
Accumulating (Acc) ETFs automatically reinvest any dividends back into the fund — your share price grows silently without you receiving cash. Distributing (Dist) ETFs pay dividends directly to your bank account as cash. For wealth building, accumulating ETFs are superior because dividends compound automatically without triggering an immediate tax event. For retirees needing regular income, distributing ETFs may be more appropriate.
Do I need to rebalance an ETF savings plan? ▾
If you hold a single MSCI World ETF, no rebalancing is needed — the ETF automatically maintains its internal weights. If you hold multiple ETFs (such as 80% MSCI World and 20% Emerging Markets), check annually whether your allocation has drifted significantly. If it has, direct your next few months of savings plan contributions entirely toward the underweighted ETF to restore balance — no selling required.
